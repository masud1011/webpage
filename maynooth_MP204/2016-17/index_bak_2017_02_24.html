Material covered in Class

I point to relevant chapters in Prof. Nash's Notes and in Vol. II of the Feynman lectures.
Of course, equivalent material is available in many other textbooks.

Electric Currents.
In the 4th week we begin our study of electric currents.
See Chapter 13 in Feynman lectures Vol. II.   Nash notes: chapter III.

Applications of Gauss' law.
In the 3rd and 4th weeks we've been studying Gauss' law and its applications. This is discussed in most standard texts; the particular examples discussed may vary.
In Feynman lectures Vol. II, Chapter 5 is highly recommended reading.   In Nash notes, this is chapter II.

Electric flux. Gauss' dielectric flux theorem (a.k.a. Gauss' law).
At the end of the second week, we began our study of Gauss' dielectric flux theorem.
In Feynman lectures Vol. II, this discussion of flux begins in Chapter 4 Section 5, and continues through Chapter 5.   Nash-notes covers this material in Chapter 2.

Coulomb's Law, Electric Fields, Electric Potentials.
In the first three classes, we have introduced most of the material of chapter 1 of Nash-notes. The introduction to the electric potential in Nash-notes Chapter 1 Section 3 is more detailed than we had time for in class; you might want to read this carefully.
In Feynman lectures Vol. II, you will find similar material in the first 4 sections of Chapter 4.

Overview and Background.
In Feynman lectures Vol. II, Chapter 1 gives an overview of what we will learn this semester.
Chapters 2 and 3 introduces grad-div-curl and vector integration. This is essential background for MP204; it is assumed that you are familiar with most of this material. In any case, working through these chapters will probably help you greatly in MP204.

Material, sources


Lecture notes from a previous year

MP204 lecture notes of Prof. Charles Nash --- this is roughly the material to be covered in the module, with some additions. It is recommended that you work through these notes, and in addition spend significant time working through at least one textbook.


Textbooks

There are many, many textbooks on introductory electromagnetism or electrodynamics. You are strongly encouraged to work through one or more textbooks.

In particular, I will point to chapters/sections in the Feynman lectures (Volume II), which are free to read on
this website.
The material we will cover in MP204 is mostly contained within the first 20 chapters of Volume II. (Specifically: Chapters 1, 4--6, 13--18, 20.)

I point to Feynman Vol. II because it is freely available to read. However, the material is very standard and has not changed much in the past 50 years. You will find the same topics covered in many other texts.

Other texts:

  • Griffiths, Introduction to Electrodynamics.

  • Purcell, Electricity and Magnetism.

  • Panofsky & Phillips, Classical Electricity and Magnetism.

  • Grant & Phillips, Electromagnetism.

  • Fleisch, A Student's Guide to Maxwell's Equations.

  • Edminister & Nahvi, Schaum's Outline of Electromagnetics.

  • Shankar, Fundamentals of Physics II: Electromagnetism, Optics, and Quantum Mechanics.

Notation

We will use SI (also called MKS or MKSA) units. Note that many equations look quite different when written in Gaussian (or CGS) units. When reading a textbook, be sure to watch out for which units that text is using.

Notations vary. I will mostly try using the same notations as in Prof. Nash's notes, but will not always succeed. You anyway need to be able to read and learn from multiple sources using different notations for the same physical quantities.
