Continuous Assessment and the purpose of problem sets

I plan to assign one problem set every week. They will be posted on this webpage.

The assignments will be due on tuesdays at the beginning of the class at noon.

I will not be able to mark all the assignments; some assignments will go un-marked. I will not announce beforehand which. This means: it is in your advantage to work on each problem set, whether or not a particular one gets marked.

Without working on each assignment set, you are likely to get lost quickly, as the module will build on the assignments and assume that you have learned the material you are supposed to learn through doing the assignments.

Assignment marks (`continuous assessment') will be counted toward the final module mark only if they are to the students' advantage.
(The policy is module-dependent and varies within the Mathematical Physics department. In some modules, continuous assessment is `compulsory'.)

The primary purpose of the problem sets is really that they should help you learn the material at an optimal rate. It will be assumed that you are attempting every problem set. If you are comfortable doing all the problems during the week and then want to tackle additional problems, please let me know with a brief email and I will try to send you some further optional problems to work on.

Material, sources

Special Relativity challenges our intuition and takes effort to digest; so I strongly suggest trying to read a fair bit every week.

For example, you could aim to work through two sections of Nash's notes (below) every week, or a similar amount of work from another text.


Nash notes:

MP352 lecture notes of Prof. Charles Nash --- this has a lot of overlap with the material to be covered in the module. Unfortunately, the ordering is completely different.


Textbooks:

There are many textbooks covering special relativity, our library carries a number. Special relativity is also covered in many textbooks on classical mechanics or electrodynamics/electromagnetism, and sometimes summarized in the beginning of texts on general relativity or particle physics. I list a selection below.

(I omit publisher and publication date; should be easy enough to find.)

  • Chapters 12 -- 14 of:     Kleppner & Kolenkow, Introduction to Mechanics, 2nd ed.
    There are several copies of the book in the university library. Very pedagogical. Covers the more basic half or so of what we will do in this module.

  • Chapters 15 -- 17 of:     The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Volume I
    A classic. Free to read online, on this website.

  • Chapter 1 of:     Landau & Lifshitz, The Classical Theory of Fields
    This is Volume 2 of the famous `Course of Theoretical Physics'.

  • Chapter 10 of:     Griffiths, Introduction to Electrodynamics

  • Chapters 11 -- 13 of:     Morin, Introduction to Classical Mechanics

  • Chapter 7 of:     Goldstein, Classical Mechanics
    Concise treatment of some of the more advanced aspects.

  • Chapters 1 -- 4 (and bits of chapters 5,6) of:     Woodhouse, Special Relativity
    More mathematical.

  • Chapters 1 -- 5 of:     Rindler, Relativity: Special, General, and Cosmological

  • Chapters 29 -- 34 of:     Greiner, Classical Mechanics: Point Particles and Relativity

  • Bais, Very Special Relativity: An Illustrated Guide
    A very special and worthwhile read. Avoids formalism and teaches through pictures.

  • Steane, Relativity Made Relatively Easy

  • Zee, Einstein Gravity in a Nutshell
    This book targets General Relativity, but on the way treats Special Relativity in detail. Great fun to read: many puns and anecdotes.
